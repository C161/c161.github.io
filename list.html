I. Prerequisite

What this is and what this isn't

As you can see, this is more of a to-do list and not a full guide on how to protect yourself. If you need detailed instructions and more background knowledge, I can highly recommend The Hitchhiker‚Äôs Guide to Online Anonymity / Tor. Before you start, there are some things you need to know. During every step in the process, you should fully understand what you are doing and what the implications and limitations of your actions are. It is impossible to have a set-up that is completely secure and anonymous. Everything can be breached by an adversary if time and resources are on their side. Since you as a human are the weakest link in the chain, you should also have a plan, what to do if things are starting to go sideways.
This list does not follow a strict threat model, so that you can decide how deep you want to dive into the privacy rabbit hole. Some things might contradict each other, so be aware and think twice before implementing (especially in regard to balancing security vs uniqueness of your fingerprint).

Your computer hardware

  • Prefer secure and "Respects Your Freedom" rated hardware from vendors such as Vikings, Technoethical or Libiquity
  • Hardware that ships with Linux preinstalled: Insurgo, NitroKey, ThinkPenguin, System76 and Purism
  • Hardware from Purism and NitroKey can be bought with tamper-evident packaging (tamper-evident tape, bag, glitter nail polish)
  • Prefer to buy your hardware anonymously from small retailers with cash and not online (unless bought with Monero over Tor)

Your processor

Since the Intel ME and the AMD PSP are possible privacy and security threats, you can:

  • Check if you can use me_cleaner to deactivate the ME as far as that is possible
  • Use an AMD processor, since the AMD PSP can be deactivated in some BIOSes/UEFIs
  • To avoid IntelME and AMD PSP completely use devices from RaptorCS (POWER9 processor), Pine64 (Rockchip / Allwinner processor) or old models of Intel / AMD processors

Your router

Routers brands usually have unique configurations and settings, so I will list many different options here. Probably not all of them are available to you.

  • Buy your router instead of renting one from your ISP
  • Use open source firmware like OpenWrt
  • Keep your firmware up-to-date
  • Change the default password of your router
  • Turn on your firewall and enable automatic updates
  • Disable WPS, PING, Telnet, SSH, UPnP, HNAP and Remote Administration
  • Disable Cloud-Based Management
  • Prohibit communication between different devices
  • Whitelist MAC addresses
  • Change the DNS server of your router to one that respects privacy
  • Change the local & the default IP address of your router
  • Don't open ports unless you know what you are doing
  • Turn off your router when you don't use it
  • Use an HTTPS web interface instead of just an HTTP one
  • You might want to consider using Ethernet instead of Wi-Fi / Tor

    • If you use Wi-Fi:

  • Make your SSID as impersonal as possible so that you are not immediately identified
  • add _nomap to the SSID to opt-out of the SSID data collection from Google
  • Use WPA3 or WPA2 encryption, since all others (WEP and WPA) are not secure
  • Change your SSID from time to time
  • Use a guest Wi-Fi for friends' devices or for IoT devices if you have to use them
  • Use VLANs to isolate devices further

IV. In the BIOS / UEFI of your computer

Again, I will list many options here, but not all of them will probably be available to you:

  • Protect your BIOS / UEFI with a strong passphrase
  • Enable virtualization to be able to use virtual machines
  • Enable HDD / SSD password if the feature is available
  • Disable Bluetooth completely if you can
  • Disable the Webcam and Microphone if you can
  • Disable USB / HDMI or any other port (Ethernet, FireWire, SD card ‚Ä¶) if you can
  • Disable Intel ME or AMD PSP if you can
  • Disable boot from CD / DVD / flash drive if you can
  • Disable Secure Boot if you intend to use Qubes OS as they don't support it out of the box / Keep it on if you use Linux or Windows

Installation and system settings in Linux

  • Verify that the checksum of the installation ISO is correct
  • Do the installation offline
  • Don't use personal information (name, device model...) as names
  • Use strong passphrases for your root / user account (over 15 characters)
  • Choose a "minimal installation"
  • Create different file partitions for /usr, /home, /var and /tmp
  • Use full disk encryption when installing a new distro
  • Fill your drive with random data
  • Don't connect accounts to the operating system
  • Don't allow any data collection if prompted
  • Disable Bluetooth and location services if you don't need them
  • Enable automatic updates
  • Set a privacy-respecting DNS server in the network settings (at best your own)

  • Open downloaded files only without internet connection
  • Don't log into accounts over Tor
  • Don't share personal information
  • Don't install Add-ons, plugins or themes / Tor
  • Don't click on any unknown .onion links
  • Don't visit HTTP pages (unless it is an .onion page)

  • Never use full screen (best is not to resize)

    XVII. Passwords / Passphrases

    • Use passphrases instead of passwords
    • Passphrases should contain 15 characters or more

      • ‚Üí they should consist of upper and lower case letters, numbers and characters

    • Use some kind of formula
    • Don‚Äôt use 4-digit PINs
    • Avoid using only multiple words (because of dictionary attacks)
    • Don't use the same passphrase for more than one account
    • Change default passphrases only if there was a hack
    • Don't share your passphrases with anyone
    • Don't let websites/apps store passphrases
    • Use hardware tokens
    • Don't use security questions

      • ‚Üí if you have to: don't answer honestly

    • Check regularly if passphrases are listed on Have I Been Pwned or DeHashed
    • Store the most important passphrases, e.g. for online banking offline or on devices that you rarely use
    • Don't log into accounts on devices that you don't own

    XX. VPNs

    • A VPN alone will make you neither more secure nor anonymous
    • Use a VPN if you torrent, want to get access to geo-blocked content or if you are on a public Wi-Fi
    • Don't use free VPNs (except for Proton VPN)
    • Compare VPN-providers with Techlore's VPN Chart or with the comparison from That One Privacy Guy

      • For strong security, the VPN provider should:

    • Offer anonymous payment (cash/Monero)
    • Utilize strong encryption
    • Have Open Source clients
    • Host their own servers
    • Use bare-metal servers
    • Use a strong VPN protocol like WireGuard
    • Use perfect forward secrecy
    • Have a good privacy history
    • Have a clear privacy policy

    • Check your public IP and your DNS before and after you connect
    • Check for DNS leaks
    • Check for WebRTC leaks
    • Since IPV6 can leak data, you may want to disable it
    • Check the settings of your VPN to see if you can cut off your device from other devices on the same network

    • If you are an advanced user, try to set up your own VPN with a VPS
    • Don't use a VPN with Tor unless you know what you are doing

    XXV. Smart devices

    XXVI. Using public Wi-Fi

    • Don't allow your devices to auto-connect to networks
    • Change your MAC-address before connecting to a network
    • Use a VPN or Tor ‚Üí not only as your browser but for the whole device
    • To see if it is working check your public IP and your DNS before and after connecting
    • IF you use a VPN see if you can cut your device off from other devices
    • Don't use devices containing sensitive data in public networks
    • Avoid sensitive things like banking or logging into your email account
    • Disable file sharing
    • Only visit sites using HTTPS
    • Instead of using a public Wi-Fi you could create a hotspot from your phone for your computer

    XXVII. Advanced threat protection

    • Don't conduct sensitive activity while connected to an untrusted/unsecure power line to prevent power line leaks
    • Don't use your devices in front of a camera that could be compromised
    • Use your devices in a soundproofed room to prevent sound leaks (at the best case with no windows)
    • Use your devices in Faraday cage to prevent electromagnetic leaks
    • Don't talk sensitive information where lightbulbs could be observed from outside
    • Buy your devices from different/unpredictable/offline places (shops) where the probability of them being infected with malware is lower
    • Don't let anyone access your air-gapped computers
    • Trust no one
